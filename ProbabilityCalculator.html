A simple calculator that shows how to solve some simple probability problems taken from the real world.

Problem 1

The deck of cards

We have a deck of $n$ cards and we want to know the probability of finding an exact card of which we have $c$ copies after n draws. In this problem we do not consider the possibility of a mulligan.
Basically we can rewrite this problem as a simple random variable. The random variable that we need is $X$ and it is a geometric distribution, with the exception that the probability of success increases as each failure occurs. We try to give a formal definition to this random variable in the following way:
First, let \[ p = \frac{c}{n}\] be the probability of finding the card as the first card of the deck. The definition follows from the classic definition of probability.
Then, we can work out how this probability changes as a card different from the one we are looking for is extracted. Let's call $k$ the number of extractions that have already been performed, then \[ p_k = \frac{c}{n-k}\] is the probability that the k-th extraction is a success.
To find out, though, the probability of never having a success in any of the extraction prior to the x-th we need to consider the following distribution: \[P(X = x) = p_x\prod_{i=0}^{x - 1} (1 - p_i)\] which means that we fail in each and every attempt prior to $x$, but then we get a success. So $x$ is the first success.
The previous graph is not really useful since most of the time in card games we need to draw a card by some turn to win the game. Just knowing the various probabilities is pointless then. We instead should use the sum of these probabilities to give us an indication about the turn in which we will be able to perform the action that will make us win the game. Such a sum, in probability theory is called a "Cumulative Distribution" and is sometimes written as $P(X \le x)$ which indicates the probability that the random variable $X$ has a value that is less or equal to $x$.

Problem 2

The Impossible Button Game

A new game is out: its name is "The Impossible Button Game"! In this game you have to press a big red button. If you press it you advance to the next level and there are 100 levels. But wait, there's a catch! Every time you pass a level the "difficulty" increases (as in all good games) reducing by 1% the probability of the button to activate the following time. If you press a button and it works, it doesn't immediately send you to the next level, you have to finish all your attempts. At the very beginning of the game you are given a choice: how many attempts do you want to try and press the button at each level? Be careful: if you choose a too low number you are never going to win, choose a too high number and you are going to wait for a very long time before you advance to the next level.
We are now going to study the problem a bit and we'll see how different values for our choice will change our probability of success.
First of all let's revise the geometric distribution: Given the random variable $G = \mathcal{G}(p)$ $$ p(k) = P(G = k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p $$ $$ P(k) = P(G \le k) = 1 - (1 - p)^k $$ The probability mass function tells us the probability of winning after $k-1$ failures. The cumulative distribution function, instead, tells us how likely we are to hit a win by $k$ or less trials.
The game is basically a bunch of geometric random variables stacked together and played sequentially: let's calle $$ G_1 = \mathcal{G}(1) $$ $$ G_2 = \mathcal{G}(0.99) $$ $$ G_3 = \mathcal{G}(0.98) $$ $$...$$ $$ G_{100} = \mathcal{G}(0.01) $$
The probabilities of success are in $Ps = (1, 0.99, 0.98, ..., 0.01)$ Where $p_i$ is the $i$th element of $Ps$
For the sake of simplicity let's call $Qs = (0, 0.01, 0.02, ..., 0.99)$ the probabilities of failure. Where $q_i$ is the $i$th element of $Qs$
Let's now call $W(k)$ a cumulative distribution function that tells us the probability of winning the whole game (all 100 levels) using $k$ trials for each level.
Then $W(k)$ can be written like this: $$ W(k) = P(G_1 \le k)P(G_2 \le k)...P(G_{100} \le k) = $$ $$ P_1(k)P_2(k)...P_{100}(k) = $$ $$ \prod_{i = 1}^{100} P_i(k) = \prod_{i = 1}^{100} 1 - (1-p_i)^k = $$ $$ \prod_{i = 1}^{100} 1 - q_i^k$$
